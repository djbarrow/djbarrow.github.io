Climate History:Atmosphere: Current Martian Atmospheric Data

Surface atmospheric pressure: ~6.1 mb (about 1/150th that of Earth's)
Surface gas density: ~0.020 kg/m3
Atmospheric scale height: 11.1 km
Average temperature: ~210 K (-63 degrees Celsius)
Wind speeds: 2-7 m/s (summer), 5-10 m/s (fall), 17-30 m/s (dust storm)

Composition:

Major gases (percentage by moles): Carbon Dioxide (CO2) - 95.32%
Nitrogen (N2) - 2.7%
Argon (Ar) - 1.6%
Oxygen (O2) - 0.13%
Carbon Monoxide (CO) - 0.08%
Minor (parts per million): Water (H2O) - 210
Nitrogen Oxide (NO) - 100
Neon (Ne) - 2.5
Hydrogen-Deuterium-Oxygen (HDO) - 0.85
Krypton (Kr) - 0.3
Xenon (Xe) - 0.08

Current martian atmosphere is sparse, cold, dry, and nearly all carbon dioxide. It is a dismal, barren environment characterized by strong winds which can exceed 100 miles per hour, causing vicious dust storms which erode surface structures. These winds are driven by seasonal heating and are  most common when Mars is closest to the Sun (in its relatively eccentric orbit). Such strong dust storms kick up extremely large dust clouds which then combine with carbon dioxide ice in the air, creating an ambient "haze" in the atmosphere. Incident light from the Sun is refracted by these smog-like clouds, giving Mars its characteristic gray to yellowish sky, so different from Earth's blue.

Like Earth, Mars' rotational axis is not perpindicular to the plane of its rotation around the sun. Because of this Mars experiences "seasons" analogous to Earth's, characterized by temperature changes which trigger large winds, hazier horizons and many cirrus-like clouds. There are no water clouds in the Martian sky; because of the temperature and pressure,  water passes straight from solid to gas and back. Mars does have a very thin ozone layer at its thickest above the poles; it is only 1/50th as thick as Earth's. It is not thick enough to shield the planet from radiation bombardment or prevent the escape of volatiles into space. Current data suggests that much of Mars' past atmosphere has been lost through this process.


(a typical, hazy martian sunset)

The martian atmosphere 3.8 billion years ago was thicker, warmer, wetter and more conducive to life.  The composition of Mars' atmosphere differs from Earth's not only in the relative composition of gases but also in the ratio of isotopes of a given substance (e.g: deuterium/hydrogen, argon38/argon36, xenon136/xenon130), with a higher relative amount of the heavier isotope in every case. This tells us that the lighter isotopes have been preferentially lost. The explanation for this is simple: lighter isotopes experience less gravitational attraction to the planet and have been lost faster into space than the heavier ones. Therefore Mars must have once had a much thicker atmosphere.

Crater erosion patterns provide strong clues as to what the atmosphere was like just over 3.5 billion years ago(crater erosion stopped at that time). Craters smaller than 15km from that period have been obliterated almost entirely and the rest are substantially eroded. The pattern of their erosion indicates a surface drainage flow of some kind. This tells us that the atmosphere must have been significantly warmer, warm enough to allow substances, at least temporarily, to assume liquid form.  Scientists postulate [or we postulate] that CO2 was released in sufficiently high amounts (even as high as 1-2 bars pressure) to create a greenhouse effect and warm the planet, allowing temporary liquid water to flow and erode the surface of Mars.  It is this postulated existence of ancient liquid water which holds out the possibility of life on Mars.

Whatever the ancient case was, it is quite clear that most of Mars' volatiles and atmospheric gases have been lost to space over the epochs, leaving only a thin wisp of what once existed.
